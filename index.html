Introduction

This document is a guide to the grammar and script of the language {Tôttao Nódare} (Nedarian) of the Squid People ({Bǎntoa Nódare}), specifically the variant spoken by immigrants on Earth.

All off-planet variants of Nedarian descend from a single language known as {Aquatic Nedarian}, which was spoken underwater. Because of the limitations of sound transmission in water, the language boasted some unusual features, namely an atrophic consonantal inventory, no distinction of any vowel articulatory quality whatsoever (somewhat imprecisely termed a ``one-vowel language''), several loud implosive clicks, and a very complex system of tonality and length. In effect, Aquatic Nedarian was an almost completely musical language.

As Nedarians progressively shifted to ground for daily life, many of these features mutated to redistribute themselves into the new space allowed by the sound transmission properties of air. All Nedarian languages retain a ``one-vowel'' system, as distinguishing vowel quality is biologically difficult for this species, but much of the ``musical'' aspects transformed into a more sophisticated consonantal inventory. All in all, the resulting {Surface Nedarian} family features languages with reasonable consonant inventories, "one-vowel", tonal systems with no length distinction, and simple phonotactics with open syllables.

After contact with alien species, one can identify a general {Spacebound Nedarian} family spoken by off-planet Squid People. This then underwent significant fragmentation as the space-bound population spread across star system. Because of the specifics of their culture, creoles are unlikely, and influences of external languages on Nedarian is suppressed. Nevertheless, the family of {Colonial Nedarian} dialects evidences the effects of contact with the Reticulan language, and specifically {Earth Colonial Nedarian} ({ECN}) has developed unique features due to the isolation of Earthan society.

Explain allophony

Speech is divided in distinct words; the word is intended to include not just the base form but also all kind of postpositions. That the word boundary is not interrupted is specified with a dash instead of a space. Each word is composed of syllables which are strictly of the form CV, where C is one of consonant clusters above and V is simply a. The first syllable's consonant can be any from the "Medio-initial" or "Initial" columns (if C is -, then this means the word just starts with vowel), while for all following syllables one chooses from the "Medio-initial" and "Medial" columns. To summarise these rules more simply, we can reword them as follows:

  • Consonant clusters are always separated by a vowel.
  • Two vowels are never adjacent within a word.
  • Words always end in a vowel.
  • Words cannot start in a geminated consonant

There are some more precise rules on which consonants can be in adjacent syllables, but these are only applied in the base form themselves and can be broken by the addition of postpositions.

The structure of tones is much more regular than it may appear. Straight tones consist of a uniform pitch shift from an initial to a final pitch. The bounced tones are composed of the same movement, which is then followed by a short leap into a middle pitch and then back to the regular end pitch.

The tone has to be properly distributed through the syllables of the word, which we remind also includes any postpositions. The rule is that if possible each syllable is assigned a steady pitch, and gliding syllables are resorted to only when necessary. Straight tones offer no difficulty, as each word is at least two-syllables long. For the DIP and PEAK tones, in two-syllable words the first one is at the start pitch and the second is a glide, while with three and more there is no issue. For the TUMBLE and JUMP tones, in two-syllable words one resorts to two glides, and in three-syllable words the first two are steady and the last one is the glide. Detail exceptions

Grammar

Syntax

All Nedarian languages display a specific flavor of strong ergativity that is comparatively rare in other natural languages, and is known historically as {aquatic ergativity}. This means in practice that the usual distinction between Subjects (S) and Direct Objects (O) of phrases does not really exist, and rather one distinguishes Agents (A) and Patients (P). Agents correspond to the subject of transitive clauses, while patients are either objects of transitive clauses or subject of intransitive ones. For example, in

{kǎrnote} {bánto-wa} {tsȁne}
apples person eat
The person ate the apples

(where we have omitted all extra grammatical information for simplicity), "the person" is the Agent, and "the apples" are the patient. But in an intransitive clause like

{hânto} {tsâttarne}
sun shines
The sun shines

"the sun" is the Patient. This reflects in the whole language in the relationship the agents and patients have to the verbs. The general tendency to keep in mind is that clauses are generally thought of as composed of a patient and a verb (so, PV structure), with the verb being by default the most important component (head), and that agents are considered to be optional markings or properties of the verb itself. In fact, verbs themselves agree in gender with the patient, but are also inflected optionally for the person of the agent. This philosophy only partially explains the extremely bizarre default word order of {Object-Subject-Verb} (that is, Patient-Agent-Verb) for transitive clauses. The complete structure of the default order is as follows:

(Indirect Object) - Patient - (Agent) - Verb

However, since, as we will see, nouns will be marked for their role in a sentence, the order can be rearranged without risk of ambiguity. One simple motivation for rearrangement is emphasis. Colonial Nederian emphasises elements by placing them in final position. Note the following examples:

{bánto-wa} {tsȁne} {kǎrnote}
person eat apples
The person ate _the apples_
{kǎrnote} {tsȁne} {bánto-wa}
appleseatperson
_The person_ ate the apples

By placing the Patient and Agent respectively in final position, one brings emphasis. The meaning is roughly ``it is specifically the apples that the person ate'' and ``it was the person specifically that ate the apples''. For placing emphasis on the verb which is usually already final there's a weirder setup:

{kārnote} {bǎnto-wa} {tsȁne-na}
the apples the person eat
The person _ate_ the apples

This introduces a confusing construct that we will see later, known as the antipassive. This essentially shifts the agent into a patient and the patient into an indirect. Performing this maneuver where it is not strictly necessary constitutes in itself emphasis for the verb.

The marking of roles of nouns is through the use of case, which itself is determined through tones, as explained later. By default, Patients will be marked in the {Absolutive} case, while Agents will take on the {Dominant} case (which encompasses both the usual Ergative and Genitive cases). Reprising the first examples:

Number

It's important to understand that the base form of a noun already carries an inherent number. This can be as follows
  • Singular, which is the case for proper nouns and some common nouns. For example {kanyatsa} ``arm'', can be pluralised as {kanyatsa-she} ``arms''.
  • Collective, which is the case for {most} common nouns. For example {banta} ``persons'' is inherently plural, referring to some unspecified amount of people or to people in general, and by modifying with a singulative postposition we have {banta-wa} ``person''.
  • Mass nouns, like {gaxa} ``water''.
  • Set nouns can be confusing. These refer to a set of objects that usually come in a predetermined number, almost always a pair. The set noun is typically the simplest form, and it itself can be singular or collective, with a preference for the latter. For example, {tsadara} ``pairs of eyes'' is a Collective Pair noun. It can be singularised as {tsadara-wa} ``pair of eyes'' in which case it refers to a single pair, or it can be split as {tsadara-nte} ``eye'' which then becomes a singular noun for the single element, which in turn we can pluralise as {tsadara-nta-she} ``eyes''.
In addition, one can employ other number specifiers to singular or collective nouns.

Collective nouns can have irregular singularisations, usually involving reduplication. For example {dakawasa} ``coins'' singularises as {dakawasa-sa} ``coin'' instead of the ungrammatical {dakawasa-wa}. These irregular forms are specified in the lexicon. Set nouns are always masculine, and the split forms are always feminine. Non-set nouns can be masculine or feminine, and all other number postpositions do not affect gender.
