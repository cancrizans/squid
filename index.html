{-} represents a null consonant. We remark %!% is a click sound, and ː represents gemination of the consonants.

The vowel inventory is much reduced: there is only one vowel, rendered phonetically as %a% and in romanisation as {a}. More specifically, no frontness, openness nor roundess are distinguished phonemically - essentially all vowel sounds are considered to be valid realisations. The missing information is instead carried almost entirely in tone.

Speech is divided in distinct words; the word is intended to include not just the base form but also all kind of postpositions. That the word boundary is not interrupted is specified with a dash instead of a space. Each word is composed of syllables which are strictly of the form CV, where C is one of consonant clusters above and V is simply a. The first syllable's consonant can be any from the "Medio-initial" or "Initial" columns (if C is -, then this means the word just starts with vowel), while for all following syllables one chooses from the "Medio-initial" and "Medial" columns.

To summarise these rules more simply, we can reword them as follows:

  • Consonant clusters are always separated by a vowel.
  • Two vowels are never adjacent within a word.
  • Words always end in a vowel.
  • Words cannot start in a geminated consonant, nor in {nt-}

There are some more precise rules on which consonants can be in adjacent syllables, but these are only applied in the base form themselves and can be broken by the addition of postpositions.

The structure of tones is much more regular than it may appear. Straight tones consist of a uniform pitch shift from an initial to a final pitch. The bounced tones are composed of the same movement, which is then followed by a short leap into a middle pitch and then back to the regular end pitch.

The tone has to be properly distributed through the syllables of the word, which we remind also includes any postpositions. The rule is that if possible each syllable is assigned a steady pitch, and gliding syllables are resorted to only when necessary. Straight tones offer no difficulty, as each word is at least two-syllables long. For the DIP and PEAK tones, in two-syllable words the first one is at the start pitch and the second is a glide, while with three and more there is no issue. For the TUMBLE and JUMP tones, in two-syllable words one resorts to two glides, and in three-syllable words the first two are steady and the last one is the glide.

In romanisation, tone is represented by diacritics on the first vowel of the word, as follows:

Equivalently, we will refer to the tones also by the initial of their names, so respectively H F D T R L J P.

Syntax

LANGNAME is a strongly head-final language. The standard word order in a phrase is Object-Subject-Verb. In general, adpositions and adjectives follow nouns, and determiner phrases precede them.

Alignment is of the ergative-absolutive type. Specifically, in intransitive phrases of the type S Vintr, the subject is in the Absolutive case, while in a transitive O S Vtrans phrase, the object will be in the Absolutive while the subject will be marked with the Dominant case (which encompasses Ergative and Genitive). For example:

{kàcha-za} {Ǎyaka}
ABL.F.house-in ABS.F.Ayaka
%Fkachaza Dayaka%
Ayaka is in the house
{kǎrnata} {Áyaka} {tsàna}
ABS.F.apples(COLL) ERG.F.Ayaka eat
%Dkarnata Rayaka Ftsana%
Ayaka ate the apples

Number

It's important to understand that the base form of a noun already carries an inherent number. This can be as follows
  • Singular, which is the case for proper nouns and some common nouns. For example {kanyatsa} ``arm'', can be pluralised as {kanyatsa-shi} ``arms''.
  • Collective, which is the case for {most} common nouns. For example {banta} ``persons'' is inherently plural, referring to some unspecified amount of people or to people in general, and by modifying with a singulative postposition we have {banta-wa} ``person''.
  • Mass nouns, like {gaxa} ``water''.
  • Set nouns can be confusing. These refer to a set of objects that usually come in a predetermined number, almost always a pair. The set noun is typically the simplest form, and it \emph{itself} can be singular or collective, with a preference for the latter. For example, {tsadara} ``pairs of eyes'' is a Collective Pair noun. It can be singularised as {tsadara-wa} ``pair of eyes'' in which case it refers to a single pair, or it can be split as {tsadara-nte} ``eye'' which then becomes a singular noun for the single element, which in turn we can pluralise as {tsadara-nta-shi} ``eyes''.
In addition, one can employ other number specifiers to singular or collective nouns.
Postposition Effect/meaning Applies to
{-sha} Pluraliser Singular
{-wa} Singulariser Collective

Collective nouns can have irregular singularisations, usually involving reduplication. For example {dakawasa} ``coins'' singularises as {dakawasa-sa} ``coin'' instead of the ungrammatical {dakawasa-wa}. These irregular forms are specified in the lexicon. Set nouns are always masculine, and the split forms are always feminine. Non-set nouns can be masculine or feminine, and all other number postpositions do not affect gender.
